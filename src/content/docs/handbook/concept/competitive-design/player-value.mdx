Arena's competitive design is not extra theory around the game. It is meant to
make Arena matches more useful for the people playing them.

## At a glance

| Player-facing value | What it means | Design choices behind it |
|---|---|---|
| Stable match environment | Clearer, fixed match conditions. No OP loadouts. Less luck involved.  | Controlled formats|
| Fairer comparison | Results are compared with other results from the same kind of competitive question. | Controlled formats, rating dimensions |
| Killer-specific records | Build visible records on the killers you actually play. | Rating dimensions, competitor units |
| Character-specific rivals | Compare your results with players proving themselves on the same killer. | Rating dimensions, competitor units |
| Survivor team Competency | Build a team record together without needing to enter a tournament. | Competitor units |
| Better practice targets | See which match types, killers, you perform well in, and which ones you should practice next. | Rating dimensions, repeated evidence |
| Better matchmaking | Balanced, exciting matches. It then produces better performance evidence. | Rating dimensions, repeated evidence |
{/* | More meaningful recognition | TBD | */}

## For killer players

A killer player can build a record on the killers they actually bring into
Arena.

If you play Nurse, your Nurse results can become their own history.
If you also play Blight, Spirit, Huntress, or another killer, those records can
develop in their own histories too. That makes your profile easier to read:
players can see where you are already proven, where you are still building
evidence, and which killer you are being tested on.

This also makes rivalry more specific. A strong Nurse record means you are
measuring yourself against other Nurse players in the same kind of format, not only against the survivors you face. The comparison is
narrower, but it is more useful: it shows what you have actually demonstrated
with that killer.

## For survivor groups

A survivor group can build a record as a team.

Arena gives persistent groups a way to show what they can do together across
repeated matches. That matters because survivor strength is not only four individual skill levels.
A group has its own habits and coordination pattern. Arena
lets that shared performance become visible as a group record.

The result is a clearer competitive identity. A group can be known for the
matchups it has actually played and proven, then keep expanding that record as
it takes on more killer contexts.

## For matchmaking

Better evidence can make matchmaking more useful over time.

When Arena has repeated results in a specific format and context, it can build a
clearer estimate of competitor performance. That helps place players
against opponents closer to their current level, which should create more
competitive and exciting matches.

Those closer matches also produce better evidence. A balanced match says more
about both sides than a mismatch, so improved matchmaking and improved rating
evidence can reinforce each other.


## For practice

A controlled format gives players a clear practice target. The match conditions
are known, the restrictions are published, and the result belongs to a clear
match type.

That helps players understand where their performance is actually strong or
weak. A killer player can see which killers are established and which need more
work. A survivor group can see whether it is stable against Nurse, Blight,
Spirit, Huntress, or another killer.

The value is not just a number. It is knowing what kind of match to practice
next.
