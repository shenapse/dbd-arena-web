---
title: Competitor-Unit Attribution
description: "How Arena decides whose performance a match result should represent."
---

## Defining the competitor

Arena uses competitor to mean the stable unit whose performance a result can
responsibly represent. This is not always the same thing as an individual
account. The side, the format, and the killer context affect the rating
dimension where that competitor's result belongs.

Arena therefore does not use one universal player rating for every role and
situation. It first asks what competitive question the result can answer, then
places the result in the matching rating dimension and attributes it to the
competitor unit whose performance answered that question.

## Killer competitor

For killer-side results, the competitor is the killer player. The result is
attributed to that player within the format and killer-specific rating
dimension for the declared killer.

This matters because the killer changes the strategic problem of the match. A
player may be highly experienced on Nurse and still be learning Spirit, and a
survivor group that performs well against Nurse has not necessarily shown the
same strength against Blight. Treating those results as the same competitive
question would make the record harder to interpret.

The killer competitor model changes the question from only "How strong is this
killer player?" to "How does this player perform with this killer, under this
format, against serious opposition?"

## Survivor competitor

For survivor-side results, the competitor is a persistent survivor group. The
result is attributed to that group within the format and killer-specific
rating dimension for the killer it faced. A duo is the smallest survivor
competitor unit; an individual survivor is not one.

High-level survivor play is coordinated by nature. The team manages risk, uses
resources, and makes shared decisions. Individual skill matters, but the match
result cannot cleanly divide those connected contributions among four people.
The result is strongest as evidence about the group that produced it.

A fixed four-player party provides the clearest competitive identity: the same
lineup can play repeatedly and accumulate a more reliable team-level record. A
different membership does not inherit the original group's results. The group's
record is also specific to the killer context, because each killer demands
different preparation and decisions.

## What Arena does not treat as a competitor

Arena does not treat a universal player account, a blended killer record, or an
individual solo survivor as the rated competitor.

A universal player rating would mix killer and survivor roles, character
contexts, and team contexts into one number. A blended killer rating would hide
the difference between a player's killers. A solo-survivor rating would assign
a shared team outcome to an individual.

Requiring a persistent survivor group, at minimum a duo, preserves a stable
competitive identity across matches. Full parties provide the strongest version
of that identity, which is why Arena places the greatest design emphasis on
results accumulated by stable four-player groups.

## Solo survivors in unrated formats

This does not require every possible format to exclude solo survivors. A
future unrated format may admit solo survivors after the rated model has
produced enough history to support reliable matchmaking projections from other
rated contexts. Such a format would use those projections to form matches;
its results would not create or update a solo-survivor rating. It would be
intended for players with substantial existing rating history, not as the path
for new players to establish a rating.
