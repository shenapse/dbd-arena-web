---
title: Context-Specific Rating Dimensions
description: "Why Arena separates ratings by the competitive context each result actually represents."
---

Arena does not try to compress every result into one general DBD rating. A
single number would be easy to display, but it would hide too much of what the
match actually tested.

A rating dimension is a separate track for results that answer the same
competitive question. In Arena, that question depends on the format, the side,
the competitor, and especially the killer context. The point is not to make the
rating model more complicated for its own sake. The point is to keep different
kinds of evidence from being treated as the same thing.

## Why one rating is too vague

Dead by Daylight skill is not one flat category.

A killer player may be strong on Nurse and still be learning Spirit. A survivor
group may be well prepared against Blight but much weaker against Huntress. A
team that understands how to rotate, pre-run, and spend resources against one
killer may make different mistakes when the pressure pattern changes.

Those differences are not small details around the match. They are part of the
matchup itself. If Arena blended all of those results into one rating, the
rating would become harder to read. A win with one killer could make the player
look equally proven on another killer. A survivor group's good record against
one matchup could make the group look equally proven against every matchup.

That would make the rating faster to fill, but less honest about what the
results actually show.

## What a rating dimension means

A rating dimension is the context where a rating is allowed to collect results.
Results belong together when they are evidence about the same kind of
performance.

For example, in a rated 1v4 format, a killer player's Nurse results belong to
the Nurse dimension for that format. Their Spirit results belong to the Spirit
dimension. Those records can both describe the same player, but they do not
answer the same competitive question.

The same idea applies to survivor groups. A fixed survivor group's results
against Nurse belong to that group's Nurse-facing dimension. Results against
Blight belong to a different dimension. The group is the same group, but the
matchup being tested is different.

This makes a rating more specific. Instead of saying only "this competitor is
strong," the rating says something closer to "this competitor has produced
these results in this format, in this killer context."

## The killer context matters

The killer is not just a character choice. The killer changes the strategic
problem for both sides.

Nurse changes how distance and line of sight matter. Blight changes how quickly
pressure can move across the map. Spirit changes how survivors read information
in chase. Other killers create their own pressure patterns, mistakes, and
preparation demands.

Because of that, a result with one killer should not automatically count as
evidence for another killer. The player behind the keyboard may be the same,
but the task they performed was not the same.

Per-killer dimensions let killer players build records that match what they are
actually playing. A player can be recognized as a strong Nurse without the
system pretending that the same results prove their Spirit, Blight, or Huntress
level.

## Survivor ratings are also matchup-specific

The survivor side faces the same issue from the other direction.

A survivor group is not simply being tested on "survivor skill" in the
abstract. The group is being tested against a specific killer context. The
right rotations, rescue timings, resource trades, bodyblocks, and risk choices
can change heavily depending on the killer.

This is why Arena does not treat a survivor group's result against Nurse as
proof that the same group is equally proven against every killer. The group may
be excellent in that matchup and still need a separate record against Blight,
Spirit, Huntress, or another killer.

That separation gives survivor groups clearer practice feedback. A group can
see which contexts are established strengths and which contexts still need
work. It also gives matchmaking better evidence when choosing opponents for a
specific killer matchup.

## What this prevents

Context-specific dimensions prevent several common rating problems.

They prevent one killer from carrying another killer's reputation. A player who
wins often on Nurse should not automatically be matched as if their Doctor is
equally proven.

They prevent survivor groups from looking fully tested because of one familiar
matchup. Strong results against one killer are meaningful, but they are not the
same as strong results across all killer contexts.

They also prevent matchmaking from using vague evidence. If a match is being
formed around Blight, the useful question is not "how good is this account in
general?" It is closer to "how has this killer player performed on Blight, and
how has this survivor group performed against Blight?"

The result is not perfect certainty. It is cleaner evidence. The rating is
less likely to be inflated, diluted, or misunderstood by results from a
different competitive problem.

## How this works with Arena's other choices

Rating dimensions do not work alone. They sit between the format and the
competitor record.

The format defines the conditions of the match: rules, participants, map
context, result requirements, and other shared assumptions. The rating
dimension defines which competitive context the result belongs to within that
format. Competitor-unit attribution then decides whose record receives the
result: a killer player on a declared killer, or a persistent survivor group
against that killer context.

Repeated calibrated evidence completes the loop. A dimension becomes useful
because accepted results accumulate over time in the same context. One Nurse
match does not settle a player forever, but many Nurse results against rated
opposition can start to form a meaningful Nurse record.

## Limits and tradeoff

Separate dimensions need their own history. A new killer dimension, or a
survivor group's first matches against a killer, may be less certain until more
accepted results exist.

Arena reduces that cold-start cost without collapsing the dimensions together.
Related dimensions can be arranged into broader groups, and matchmaking can
read a thin specific dimension through that hierarchy. When there is little
direct evidence for the exact context, the MMR view can blend that specific
rating with ratings from the same broader group. Result updates can also write
through the configured hierarchy, so nearby context can help the system learn
while the specific dimension remains distinct.

Arena still accepts the underlying tradeoff. A faster rating that freely mixes
incompatible results would be easier to populate, but weaker for matchmaking
and harder for players to interpret. A more specific rating, supported by
hierarchical reads and writes where configured, gives the service a clearer
basis for future matches and gives players a clearer picture of what their
record actually represents.

Context-specific rating dimensions are therefore not a claim that Arena can
measure every part of DBD perfectly. They are a boundary around what each
rating is allowed to mean. The rating does not try to summarize all of a
player's DBD skill. It tries to describe performance in the competitive context
that produced the result.
