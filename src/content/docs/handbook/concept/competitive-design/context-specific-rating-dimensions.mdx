The obvious rating split is general Survivor rating and general Killer rating,
but those two broad ratings hide too much of what the match actually tested.

Arena avoids that ambiguity by using context-specific rating dimensions: ratings
whose meaning is narrow enough that the accumulated results support the claim
being shown.

## Why broad ratings become ambiguous

A Survivor rating and a Killer rating sound plausible, but they can make a
competitor look more broadly proven than the results actually support.

A killer player may be well tested on Nurse but almost untested on Doctor. If
those results feed one Killer rating, the player can look more broadly proven
than they really are.

The same problem exists for Survivor ratings. A survivor group may be well
tested against Trapper but almost untested against Blight. If those results feed
one Survivor rating, the group can look more broadly proven than it really is.

Broad ratings fill faster, but they also blur the claim the rating is making.
Arena treats that blur as a reliability problem: the number may be accurate
about the results it accumulated while still being vague about what those
results prove.

## Arena's answer: rating dimensions

Arena solves the broad-rating problem by splitting ratings into context-specific
rating dimensions.

A rating dimension is a separate rating track for one kind of competitive
evidence. It defines which results are allowed to accumulate together, so the
rating keeps saying one clear thing instead of mixing several different claims.

Results belong in the same dimension when they answer the same competitive
question. In Arena, that question is typically shaped by the format, side, competitor
unit, and additional competitive context. If one of those parts changes, the result may no
longer be evidence for the same rating.

The point is not to create more numbers for their own sake. The point is to
make each rating honest about what it has actually measured.

## How Killer context affects Arena ratings

In DBD, the Killer is not just a character choice. The Killer changes the
strategic problem for both sides, so Arena treats Killer context as part of the
rating dimension.

Killer-side records are specific to the declared Killer. Survivor-group records
are specific to the Killer they faced.

"Good on Nurse" is not the same claim as "good on Dredge." "Good against
Goodguy" is not the same claim as "good against every Killer." Those skills can
overlap, but Arena does not treat one as already proving the other.

## What this gives Arena

Context-specific dimensions give Arena cleaner evidence for interpretation and
matchmaking.

They let each rating record carry the exact competitive scope that produced it.
A Nurse record says something about that player's Nurse performance. A Doctor
record says something about that player's Doctor performance. Arena can read
each record as evidence for the context it actually represents.

That scope also keeps one killer's record from carrying another killer's
reputation. A player who wins often on Nurse should not automatically be
matched as if their Doctor is equally proven.

The same benefit applies to survivor groups. Strong results against one killer
are meaningful evidence for that matchup, not a substitute for strong results
across all killer contexts.

For matchmaking, this makes the input more relevant. If a match is being formed
around Blight, the useful question is not only "how good is this account in
general?" It is closer to "how has this killer player performed on Blight, and
how has this survivor group performed against Blight?"

For players, the record becomes easier to read. A rating can show where a
competitor has established evidence, and which
contexts still need work.

## Limits

Separate dimensions need their own match history. A new killer dimension, or a
survivor group's first matches against a killer, may be less certain until more
accepted results exist.

Arena accepts the tradeoff because unclear evidence is worse than thin but
honest evidence. Context-specific rating dimensions do not make Arena a perfect
measurement of DBD skill. They define what each rating is allowed to mean.

<Aside type="note" title="Hierarchical rating model">
  Technically, Arena reduces that cold-start cost without collapsing the dimensions together. When referenced rating evidence is thin, roughly speaking, we blend rating values of nearby dimensions to get an evidence richer MMR. (So, MMR does not necessarily coincide with rating.)
</Aside>
