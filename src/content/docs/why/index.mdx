Prove yourself in custom matches built to mean more.

DBD Arena runs rated custom matches for players who want serious competition
outside normal public matchmaking. You play under published rules, face rated
opponents, and build evidence that is easier to understand than a random public
match result.

It is not perfect proof of skill. Dead by Daylight is too complex for that. But
it gives both killer players and survivor teams a cleaner place to compete,
track progress, and play matches worth preparing for.

## What DBD Arena delivers

DBD Arena gives both sides a repeatable place to play serious custom matches.

You enter a named format with published rules, declared participants, service
ratings, and match conditions defined before the game starts.

DBD Arena does four main jobs:

- **Create serious matches.** Players queue for a competitive custom match, not
  a casual public lobby.
- **Make the format explicit.** The rules, queue requirements, killer, map
  context, restrictions, and result conditions are known before the match starts.
- **Track the right competitor.** Killer results belong to the specific killer
  played. Survivor results belong to the coordinated four-player team.
- **Give the service memory.** Results update ratings, and ratings help place
  both sides into future matches.

For killers, that means a place to prove what you can do with a specific killer
against coordinated survivor teams.

For survivor teams, that means a place to prove that your group can hold up
against killers who are deliberately testing themselves.

For both sides, the value is the same: better matches, published expectations,
and a rating-backed environment where wins and losses carry more weight than
they usually do in public matches.

## Why normal DbD matches are hard to read

Public matches are useful. They are good for practice, variety, fast games, and
casual play.

They are not good at answering one specific question:

**How strong were you really?**

A public match result can be hard to interpret. Was the result caused by the
map? The tile generation? The builds? The killer choice? The survivor team? A
single weak teammate? The opponent's mistakes?

Dead by Daylight is also asymmetric. Killers do not face killers, and survivors
do not face survivors. A killer player's result depends heavily on which killer
they played. A survivor result depends heavily on the full team.

That makes ordinary match results noisy. They can still be fun. They can still
teach you things. But they are often weak evidence of competitive strength.

DBD Arena exists to make that evidence cleaner.

## How DBD Arena makes results more meaningful

DBD Arena uses custom matches, published regulations, visible service ratings,
and controlled match conditions.

That matters because a result becomes more useful when fewer unrelated
explanations remain. In a public match, too many things are unknown at once. Was
the opponent actually close to your level? Did the map heavily favor one side?
Was the survivor team coordinated? Did an unusual/OP build decide the game? Should
this killer result be compared to results on a completely different killer?

DBD Arena reduces that noise in layers.

| Noise source | Why it makes results harder to read | How DBD Arena reduces it |
|---|---|---|
| Unknown opponent quality | A win over a much weaker opponent or a loss to a much stronger one says less about your current level. | Service ratings help form matches against opponents close enough to matter. |
| Killer strength mixed together | A result on one killer can be mistaken for proof about another killer, even though each killer tests different skills. | Killer rating is tracked per killer, not as one blended killer number. |
| Random survivor teammate noise | A survivor result can be dominated by one unfamiliar teammate, making it unclear whether the team actually played well together. | Survivors queue as a full four-player party, and the survivor side is rated as a group. |
| Map and rule ambiguity | The result can look like a skill gap when it was partly caused by map draw, unclear win conditions, or different expectations. | The published rule: fixed per-killer map selection, win and draw conditions, and restrictions. |
| Surprise information | A side may win because the other side prepared for the wrong situation, not because it outplayed them under known conditions. | The killer character, map selection, and match participants are revleaed before the match starts. |
| Loadout or build outliers | An extreme or unexpected build can decide the match and make the result less comparable to other matches. | General rules and per-killer balancing define what is allowed. |
| One-off randomness | A single match can be swung by mistakes, clutch moments, or unusual events, so one result alone is weak evidence. | Rating builds over repeated matches, not from one match alone. |

First, the match is played under a named regulation.
The regulation defines various requirements, including the game structure, queue-time requirement,
map pool, information revealed before a match starts.
For the 1v4 quartet regulation, survivors queue as a full party,
the killer queues with declaration of the killer character (e.g., Nurse),
the map is fixed per killer, and both sides know the killer and players before the match starts.
No uncertainty about rule, map, killer, teammate.

Second, the regulation has published rules and per-killer balancing. That makes
the result less likely to be explained by surprise conditions, unusual loadouts,
or an accidental mismatch between killer, map, and win condition.

Third, rating is tracked in the context that actually matters. A killer result
updates that player's rating for the specific killer they played. A survivor
result updates the fixed survivor group's rating for the killer they faced. The
system does not treat Nurse, Blight, Spirit, and Doctor as the same test, and it
does not pretend one survivor's solo rating explains a coordinated team result.

Fourth, matchmaking uses those ratings to form closer matches. A stomp can
happen, but it is not the goal. The goal is to pair opponents close enough that
the result gives both sides useful evidence.

This does not remove every variable. DbD still has execution, adaptation,
pressure, mistakes, and clutch decisions. Those are not noise. They are the
game.

The goal is not to make every result perfectly pure. The goal is to reduce the
usual noise enough that the result says more.

How rating works is explained in [Ratings](/handbook/ratings/), and how pairing
uses it is explained in
[How Matchmaking Works](/handbook/how-matchmaking-works/).


## Why rules and ratings matter

Rules and ratings are not there to make the service feel formal. They are there
to make the match worth taking seriously.

Rules give both sides a shared contract. You know what format you entered, what
was allowed, what counted as a result, and what kind of test the match was meant
to be.

Ratings give the service memory. A result does not disappear as a random win or
loss. It becomes part of the evidence used to place you into future matches and
show your progress in that specific context.

Together, rules and ratings turn one custom game into part of a repeatable
competitive environment. The match still has luck, pressure, and human mistakes.
But it is no longer just another pub result with too many unknowns attached.


## What killers get

Killer strength in Dead by Daylight is character-specific.

A result on Nurse does not mean the same thing as a result on Doctor. A Blight
match, a Spirit match, and a Wraith match test different things, even when the
same player is behind them.

DBD Arena keeps that distinction visible.

When you queue as killer, you declare the killer you are playing. Your result
builds evidence for that killer, against a rated survivor team, under known
rules.

Your progress is not hidden inside one blended number that treats every killer
as the same problem. It follows the killer you actually play.

That also changes who you are competing with. Your Huntress is measured in the
context of other Huntress results. Your Doctor is measured in the context of
other Doctor results. A lower-tier killer does not have to share one direct
comparison space with Nurse, Blight, or Spirit.

This gives killers a new competitive axis: not only "am I a strong killer
player?", but "how far can I push this killer against serious opposition?"

The published rules also make a broader killer pool more realistic. In public
matches, some killers can feel hard to justify when the map, team quality,
loadouts, and expectations are all uncertain. In DBD Arena, each killer enters a
known match context with fixed conditions and per-killer balancing, so more
characters can produce meaningful competitive matches.

That gives practice a clearer target. You know the kind of match you are
preparing for: the killer, the rule set, the map context, the restrictions, and
the result condition. You can practice toward that target inside or outside
Arena.

## What survivor teams get

High-level survivor play is coordinated by nature. A serious survivor result is
not only about four separate players. It is about how the team rotates pressure,
uses resources, manages risk, and makes decisions
together.

That is why DBD Arena treats the four-player survivor team as the canonical
competitor. Individual survivor skill still matters, but a match result cannot
cleanly separate one player's contribution from the team's communication, 
saves, mistakes, and shared decisions.

So the service does not pretend one survivor's rating can explain a coordinated
team result. The team queues together, plays together, and builds evidence
together.

Survivor teams get the same core value killers get: serious matches, clearer
results, and a reason to prepare.

That also gives survivor teams a clearer competitive target. You can compete
against rated killers, compare your results with other survivor teams, or play
against friends on another team and see who performs better under the same kind
of pressure.

Arena rules can also make survivor play feel meaningfully different from normal
DbD. Some regulations restrict perks, items, or in-game actions that are usually
available elsewhere. For example, a rule might prevent survivors from cleansing
a hex totem until a certain number of generators are completed.

Per-killer win conditions can change the match even further. A team may need to
play differently against one killer than another, not only because the killer's
power is different, but because the rule set asks for a different kind of
survivor decision-making.

That creates a useful practice target. Your team is not only trying to play
"good survivor." You are learning specific strategies, resource plans, risk
choices, and team habits for a known killer, map context, rule set, and result
condition.

## What this is not

DBD Arena is not official Dead by Daylight. It is a community-run service for
custom matches, and it does not affect your in-game rank, grade, MMR, or
progression.

It is not a replacement for public matches. Pubs are still useful for volume,
practice, experimentation, and casual play.

It is not a replacement for tournaments. Tournaments can produce strong proof,
but they require fixed events, limited slots, and heavier organization. DBD
Arena is meant to be more repeatable.

It is not a perfect measurement of skill. Rating is useful evidence and
matchmaking infrastructure, not a magic answer to who is best.

A public leaderboard or ranked recognition layer may be added later, but that
is a separate product surface. The current promise is rated custom matches,
stronger competition, and clearer personal or team progress.
