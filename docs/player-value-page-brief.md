This draft is input for a future webpage writer or designer. It is not the final
page. It defines the message, audience, section intent, and sample copy for a
page that attracts serious Dead by Daylight players before explaining the
service mechanics.

## Primary audience

The page should speak to experienced DbD players, especially players with enough
hours to care about serious match quality and credible comparison.

Killer mains are the first hook. The format is easiest to understand from their
side: choose a killer, face a serious survivor team, and build evidence for that
specific killer.

Survivor teams must still feel invited. They are not supporting actors. They are
the opponent that makes the arena meaningful.

## Page goal

The page should make a visitor interested before it asks them to understand the
system.

It should answer:

- Why should I care?
- What kind of matches will I get?
- What proof does this give me that public matches do not?
- Why is this different from pubs, ordinary comp, or generic MMR?

It should not start by explaining architecture, rating algorithms, queue
internals, or full rule details.

## Core promise

Core message:

> Built to reveal who is actually strong, with which killer, under comparable
> conditions.

Player-facing lead promise:

> Prove your killer against serious survivor teams, under rules where the result
> actually means something.

Balanced version:

> A serious DbD arena where killers prove their strength, and survivor teams
> prove they can break it.

## Message hierarchy

Lead with challenge, then proof, then recognition.

1. Challenge: find serious matches worth playing.
2. Proof: get better evidence of your strength under controlled rules.
3. Recognition: track visible service rating now; public leaderboard-style
   recognition is future/planned.

Fairness should support this message. It is infrastructure, not the headline.
Players are attracted by better matches and credible proof. Controlled rules and
ratings explain why those matches can exist.

## Killer appeal

Killer-facing message:

> Stop guessing how good your killer really is. Play rated custom matches
> against coordinated survivor teams under published rules.

What the page should communicate:

- Your killer choice matters.
- A win on one killer is not the same as a win on another.
- Rating is tracked per killer, so your result has a clearer meaning.
- The match is built to test your strength on that killer, not lucky map draw
  or surprise build advantage.

Sample section copy:

> Your Blight, your Spirit, and your Doctor are different tests. DBD Arena keeps
> those results separate. When you queue as killer, you declare the killer you
> are playing, face a rated survivor team, and build evidence for that specific
> character.

## Survivor team appeal

Survivor-facing message:

> Become the team serious killers have to beat.

What the page should communicate:

- Survivor teams are competitors, not background variables.
- The format values coordination because high-level survivor play is
  coordinated by nature.
- A full four-player party can build team-level evidence against serious
  killers.
- Survivor teams get matches worth preparing for.

Sample section copy:

> Survivor play is strongest when four players act like a team. DBD Arena treats
> that team as the competitor. Queue as a full party, face killers who are
> proving their strength on a specific killer, and prove your group can hold up
> under pressure.

## Differentiators

### Not public matches

Public matches are useful for volume, practice, and variety. They are poor at
answering "how strong am I really?" because too many conditions change at once.

DBD Arena uses custom games, published rules, and rating-backed matchmaking so
the result says more about performance.

Sample copy:

> In pubs, the question after a win is always messy. Was it the map? The team?
> The build? The matchup? Here, conditions are controlled so the result carries
> more weight.

### Not ordinary comp tournaments

Comp tournaments can produce strong evidence, but they require a fixed event,
limited slots, and larger team organization.

DBD Arena should feel more repeatable. It gives serious players a place to find
rated custom matches without treating every match like a full tournament run.

Sample copy:

> Tournament play is valuable, but it is not the only way to test yourself. DBD
> Arena gives serious players a repeatable place to play and build rating over time.

### Not just MMR

Rating is visible and useful, but rating alone is not the product. It helps form
better matches and lets players track progress.

A future public recognition layer should be separate from matchmaking rating.
Do not promise a public leaderboard until that feature has a defined contract.

Sample copy:

> Rating helps find opponents who can actually test you. It is visible feedback,
> not a magic answer to who is best. Public leaderboard-style recognition should
> come from a deliberately designed ranking layer, not from treating matchmaking
> rating as the whole story.

## Suggested page shape

### Hero

Headline options:

- Prove your killer.
- Find matches worth preparing for.
- Built to reveal who is actually strong.

Recommended hero copy:

> Prove your killer against serious survivor teams, under rules where the result
> actually means something.

Support copy:

> DBD Arena runs rated custom Dead by Daylight matches with per-killer ratings,
> full survivor teams, and published balancing. Less noise than pubs. More
> repeatable than one-off scrims. Built for players who want stronger matches
> and better evidence.

Primary CTA:

- Start playing

Secondary CTA:

- See active regulation

### Section: Find Stronger Matches

Intent: sell the challenge.

Sample copy:

> The best matches are the ones you cannot autopilot. DBD Arena pairs killers
> and survivor teams through service ratings so both sides can find opponents
> worth preparing for.

### Section: Prove A Specific Killer

Intent: explain per-killer rating without technical detail.

Sample copy:

> Killer strength is character-specific. A result on Nurse does not mean the
> same thing as a result on Doctor. DBD Arena keeps killer ratings separate, so
> your progress follows the killer you actually play.

### Section: Survivor Teams Matter

Intent: make survivor teams feel like competitors.

Sample copy:

> The format is killer-specific, but the competition belongs to both sides.
> Survivor teams queue as full parties and build team-level evidence against
> serious killers.

### Section: Rules That Make Results Mean More

Intent: explain controlled conditions as value support.

Sample copy:

> Published rules, fixed per-killer balancing, known killer selection, and
> controlled loadouts reduce the usual excuses. The service cannot remove every
> variable from DbD, but it can make results more comparable than ordinary
> matches.

### Section: Rating Now, Recognition Later

Intent: set honest expectations.

Sample copy:

> Your service rating is visible and helps place you into meaningful matches. A
> public leaderboard or recognition layer is a separate future feature. The
> launch focus is clear: better matches, better evidence, and ratings that help
> the arena work.

## Wording to use

- serious matches
- better evidence
- comparable conditions
- per-killer rating
- team-based survivor competition
- visible service rating
- less noise than pubs
- published rules
- custom matches

## Wording to avoid

- pure skill
- eliminates luck
- perfect balance
- survivor noise
- official ranking
- public leaderboard, unless clearly marked future/planned
- hidden MMR, because player rating is visible

## One-sentence summary

DBD Arena is a serious custom-match arena where killer players prove
character-specific strength, survivor teams prove coordinated strength, and both
sides get matches whose results mean more than ordinary public games.
